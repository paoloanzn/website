Intelligence needs to become something everyone should be able to achieve easily, especially in today’s society.

There’s an interesting Norwegian study, which I strongly agree with, showing that average IQ slowed down and then reversed from the 1990s onward. This shift does not appear to be driven by genetic factors, but rather by environmental ones. I think we don’t even need a study to arrive at this conclusion logically, and I don’t want to sound cliché, but it’s clear that social media, especially the kind designed for modern smartphones, is one of the main drivers of this.

In fact, I’ll go further and say that the last six years, from 2020 to today, have most likely been the most destructive period for the population’s average IQ, far more than the previous fifty years combined. COVID-19 quarantine also played a role, since it inevitably pushed a lot of people into even stronger daily social media consumption patterns.

But this is a well-known problem, and I’m not here to talk about the problem. I’m here to talk about solutions, or at least the direction in which I think we should be moving if we want to save ourselves from a danger that I believe is still seriously underestimated.

And yes, I say ourselves because I include myself in this too. In my opinion, 99% of people who say they don’t have a problem with social media are lying to themselves. In this era, you either don’t use these platforms at all, or you are enslaved by them to a meaningful degree. There is no real middle ground.

The idea of “moderate consumption” of social media is a fantasy pushed by people who like to talk about focus or productivity, but it’s a fallacy. These platforms are designed to be addictive. If you think you can overcome that mechanism through willpower alone, you are either naive or deeply delusional about your own capabilities.

Imagine these platforms as cocaine. Do you think you can stay in control if you “micro-dose” cocaine? No, you can’t. Micro-dosing cocaine would just produce a mild effect while increasing your craving for more.

I hope the concept is clear enough. I don’t want to hear anyone claim they are “immune” to social media. Most of the time, that comes from edgy people who spend their entire day on platforms they consider “not for normies,” feeling elitist while actually being even more delusional than the Instagram Reels and TikTok crowd.

At least those people openly admit they are junkies.

Yes, the Twitter/X feed is 90% pure trash content. Sorry Elon, it’s not your fault. As soon as you implement an adversarial feed-ranking system (aka The Algorithm), you can’t really expect anything different. But that’s how you build a social media platform.

So what do we do?

Simple: we build a parallel consumption system. We do not build a replacement. That is exactly where I think almost everyone attempting something similar has failed, or simply chosen to profit off you instead.

All these alternative “smart” solutions try to replace standard social media platforms. They push the narrative: “Quit TikTok and use our app to become smarter.”

That’s like saying, “Quit cocaine and start drinking our healthy electrolyte water” to a cocaine addict. His answer will probably be, “No man, I’d rather die than do that.” And for good reason.

His brain does not want to quit cocaine. Period.

His brain does not care that your app makes him “smarter.” The brain is not biologically wired to crave that. It is wired to crave reward. So all these methods that assume individual willpower is stronger than a deeply embedded biological system are complete bullshit.

A popular read such as Atomic Habits by James Clear makes this point very early on: relying on willpower is a recipe for failure.

For this reason, my proposed solution is not to convince you to uninstall TikTok or Instagram and replace them with some cringe “smart social media app.” I’m not even trying to make you spend less time on your phone in general. I have another method for that, and I’ll write about it soon.

Instead, I want you to add something.

Not replace. Add.

You can even add it on top of your braindead scrolling, if you want. Remember, the goal is not to stop using social media. The goal is to stop becoming dumber.

To do that, we’re going to use one of the oldest forms of information consumption on the internet. It has been around since the early days of the web, has been largely forgotten by younger generations, but is still active, usable, and relevant.

I’m talking about building your own RSS feed.

I’m not going to explain what RSS is, ask ChatGPT please, because the concept is simple: if I write a blog, a newsletter, or something similar, I can publish an RSS feed so that anyone can add me to a curated list of sources and automatically receive my new content whenever I publish it.

So I, the user, can build my own list of blogs and newsletters, set a daily fetch, and create my own curated feed of content whenever something new appears from those sources.

Simple, right?

This is powerful because it enables two things.

First, if curated well enough, the quality of the information you get from these kinds of sources is far better than what you get from social media. Not because the people writing it are necessarily smarter, but because, unlike social media, it does not need to be optimized for engagement or clicks.

There is no attention to capture. You either come to my blog because you want to read what I wrote, or you don’t. There is no hijacking of your attention. Nothing is shoved in front of your eyes to make you click.

Reading the content has to be your own conscious decision.

Second, the feed is deterministic, not algorithmic.

You choose what you see. If you add source X, then whenever source X publishes something new, you will see it.

That is very different from an algorithm constantly analyzing your engagement patterns in order to optimize one specific variable: the amount of time you spend on the platform.

These apps are literally designed to not let you go once you open them. If you really think about it, that is probably one of the most dystopian things ever. And yet, it’s simply reality.

Why do you think these apps never make the “following” feed the default option when you open them? Or why they make it harder to find through UI and UX choices?

Do you really think that is random?

It is far from random.
